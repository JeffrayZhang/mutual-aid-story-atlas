# Mutual Aid Story Atlas

An interactive story-map website exploring how digital tools support community care, trust, and reciprocity through mutual aid. Built with React, Vite, and Framer Motion.

**Live site:** [https://jeffrayzhang.github.io/creative-project/](https://jeffrayzhang.github.io/creative-project/)

---

## Reflection

The central challenge of this project was finding a format that could hold both an argument and an experience. The three research questions I started with—about bottom-up care, platform trust, and the difference between mutual aid and charity—are analytical by nature, but I did not want the website to read like an essay with interactive decorations bolted on. I wanted the structure itself to carry some of the meaning. That tension between making something rigorous and making something people would actually want to move through shaped most of the decisions I made.

The story-map format was my answer to that problem. By following a single fictional character, Mina, across five cities, I could ground each research question in a specific situation rather than leaving it abstract. Toronto introduces the idea that digital tools can respond faster than formal systems. São Paulo shows how coordination becomes visible through shared documents and posts. Warsaw focuses on what makes a platform feel trustworthy to someone who is new and vulnerable. Nairobi asks who holds power in different models of support. Manila ties everything together around accessibility and layered communication. Each stop adds one idea, and the interactive module at each stop lets the user test that idea rather than just read about it.

One of the hardest questions was how to represent Mina responsibly. She is a composite character, not a real person, and the cities are chosen as storytelling devices rather than documentary case studies. I chose to be transparent about that from the start, with a visible note explaining why Mina is fictional. I think this was the right call. It would have been dishonest to imply that the scenarios are direct accounts, and it would have been less useful to strip the project of narrative altogether. The fiction is a scaffold, not a mask.

The aspect of the project I believe to have been most successful is the interactive modules. Each chapter includes a different type of activity—a chat simulation, a timeline builder, a trust-signal picker, a power-sorting exercise, and an outreach-stack planner—and each one asks the user to make choices that surface the chapter's core lesson. I think these work because they are not quizzes with a single right answer. They are structured enough to teach something but open enough that different paths through them still produce useful insight. The chat scene in Toronto, for example, shows that asking for help in a mutual aid space is different from filling out an intake form, regardless of which option the user picks.

Building the site also changed how I think about digital citizenship. Before this project, I associated the concept mostly with online safety, misinformation, and responsible posting. Working through the mutual aid material pushed me toward a broader understanding: digital citizenship is also about how people use everyday tools—group chats, shared spreadsheets, simple maps—to coordinate care and share responsibility. The technology does not have to be sophisticated. What matters is whether the design invites participation or creates new barriers. That idea now feels central to how I understand the relationship between digital tools and civic life, and it is something I would not have arrived at without building the project from the ground up.
